# Understanding the Camera in Image Processing

---

## What is a Camera?

A **camera** is a device that captures images or video by collecting light through a lens.  

In programming, we use a camera (like a **webcam** or **phone camera**) to take pictures or record video so we can:

- Analyze  
- Process  
- React to what it sees (e.g., detecting faces, colors, motion)

---

## Pixels

A **pixel** is the smallest dot in an image.

- Each pixel has a color made by mixing **Red**, **Green**, and **Blue** (RGB)
- When you zoom into a photo, you’ll eventually see little squares — those are pixels
- An image is basically a **grid of pixels**

---

## FOV – Field of View

**FOV** is how wide the camera can see.

- A **wide FOV** sees more surroundings (like a wide-angle lens)  
- A **narrow FOV** zooms into a small area (like a telescope)  

> Think of FOV as the camera’s **eyesight width**

---

## Resolution

**Resolution** tells how many pixels the image contains.

- Format: `width × height` (e.g., **640×480**)  
- 640 pixels wide and 480 pixels tall  
- Higher resolution = more detail  
- But also = larger file size and more processing required

---

## FPS – Frames Per Second

**FPS** indicates how many images the camera captures per second during video recording.

- 30 FPS → 30 frames per second → **smooth** video  
- 60 FPS → even **smoother**  
- Higher FPS = better motion clarity, but **higher processing load**

---

## Aspect Ratio

**Aspect ratio** is the shape of the image, based on width and height ratio:

- **4:3** → Square-like  
- **16:9** → Wide (like most YouTube videos)

It tells us the **proportion** of the image, not the number of pixels.

---

## Focal Length

**Focal length** determines how zoomed-in the camera view is.

- **Small focal length** → Wide view, objects appear smaller  
- **Large focal length** → Zoomed-in view, objects appear larger  

It is usually measured in **millimeters** (e.g., **18mm**, **50mm**) in photography.
